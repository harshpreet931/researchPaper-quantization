% ABSTRACT
% =============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
\noindent Using Large language models on edge devices requires memory compression. In this paper we evaluate post training quantization (PTQ) on Apple Silicon. We test on Apple's MLX framework and Ollama across Qwen3.5 series (Q-0.8B, 2B, 4B parameters) and Phi-3 Mini (3.82B parameters) on an M4 Pro with 24GB unified memory. MLX INT4 quantization achieves 213.11, 131.86, 71.21, and 88.40 tokens/second for Qwen3.5-0.8B, Qwen3.5-2B, Qwen3.5-4B, and Phi-3 Mini respectively, with memory footprints ranging from 0.39GB to 2.20GB. Within MLX, INT4 provides 2.0$\times$ to 3.8$\times$ speedup over FP16 with 3.5$\times$--3.6$\times$ memory reduction. MMLU accuracy evaluation shows INT4 retains 92--99\% of FP16 accuracy for models $\geq$3.8B parameters, with Qwen3.5-4B dropping only 1 percentage point (80\%$\rightarrow$79\%) and Phi-3 Mini dropping 1 point (78\%$\rightarrow$77\%). MLX outperforms Ollama by 6.8$\times$ to 13.4$\times$ in throughput and provides 6.7$\times$ to 19.0$\times$ energy savings. All models fit within 8GB, enabling edge AI deployment while reducing energy costs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

LLMs have changed the natural language processing landscape, by bringing remarkable advances in text generation, reasoning, and knowledge retrieval \cite{brown2020language, openai2023gpt4}. This progress also carries quite a big environmental price. The International Energy Agency predicts that data centers will consume 945 TWh annually by 2030, which is about 3\% of global electricity with AI workloads driving most of this growth \cite{iea2025energyai}.

\subsection{Sustainability Challenge}

LLMs have quite a lot of environmental costs across their entire lifecycle. Training and inference account for most operational energy consumption in production \cite{patterson2021carbon}. As models grow larger to improve performance, energy will also increase, which creates a thin line between capability and sustainability.

For this, in Edge computing we have a solution, it offers lower latency, more privacy, and a lot of energy savings through local computation \cite{shi2016edge}. But, deploying LLMs on edge devices is hard, quite hard. Memory, compute, power everything is limited, and it also can't scale very much. A 4 billion param model in FP16 needs about 8GB just for weights and decent amount of RAM, more than what many edge devices can handle.

\subsection{Quantization Challenge}

Post Training Quantization (PTQ) has become the standard for model compression. It has the ability to reduce weight precision from 16-bit or 32-bit floating-point to 8-bit or 4-bit integers \cite{han2015deep}. Unlike Quantization Aware Training (QAT), PTQ works directly on pre-trained weights without retraining, which is ideal for deploying existing models on edge devices.

Several questions still remain open:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item Which PTQ method gives the best efficiency for models?
    \item How do different quantization strategies affect energy consumption?
    \item What is the optimal bit-width?
    \item How does the model architecture influence quantization sensitivity?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Contributions}

We will address these questions via experiments on Apple Silicon. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Quantization Analysis}: We evaluated MLX quantization (FP16, INT8, INT4) on Qwen3.5 series and Phi-3 Mini, finding INT4 provides 2.0$\times$ to 3.8$\times$ speedup over FP16 with 3.5$\times$--3.6$\times$ memory reduction.

    \item \textbf{Energy Measurements}: We measured energy consumption across all quantization levels, finding INT4 reduces energy by 2.2$\times$ to 3.9$\times$ vs FP16 within MLX, and MLX INT4 provides 6.7$\times$ to 19.0$\times$ energy savings vs Ollama.

    \item \textbf{Framework Comparison}: MLX INT4 outperforms Ollama Q4\_K\_M by 6.8$\times$ to 13.4$\times$ in throughput, with Qwen3.5-0.8B achieving 213.11 tok/s via MLX vs 27.24 tok/s via Ollama.

    \item \textbf{Accuracy Retention}: Using logit-based MMLU evaluation (100 questions, 10 subjects), we show INT4 quantization preserves 92--99\% of FP16 accuracy for models $\geq$3.8B parameters, with larger models exhibiting greater quantization resilience.
\end{enumerate}

These findings have an immediate value, as organizations can reduce inference energy costs while maintaining model accuracy.

\section{Background}

The impact of LLMs has become a critical research frontier especially in environmental sustainability. Training large models emits substantial amounts of carbon dioxide, and inference dominates operational energy consumption, accounting for approximately 60\% of total ML compute usage in production systems \cite{patterson2021carbon}. Moving workloads to the edge offers significant benefits \cite{shi2016edge}. However, edge devices have constraints: they have limited memory (8--16\,GB typical) and lower compute capabilities.

\subsection{Model Compression Techniques}

Model compression helps in reducing the memory and compute requirements of neural networks whilst keeping the accuracy almost intact \cite{cheng2017survey}. Some common approaches are:

\subsubsection{Quantization}

Quantization maps continuous floating-point values to discrete low-bit representations. For a weight tensor $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$, quantization to $b$ bits involves:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{W}_q = \text{clamp}\left(\text{round}\left(\frac{\mathbf{W}}{s}\right), -2^{b-1}, 2^{b-1}-1\right)
\end{equation}

where $s$ is the scale factor. And the dequantized approximation:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{W}} = s \cdot \mathbf{W}_q
\end{equation}

The quantization error $\|\mathbf{W} - \hat{\mathbf{W}}\|$ impacts model accuracy directly.

\subsubsection{Pruning}

In Pruning, based on importance criteria it will remove redundant parameters by setting weights to zero. For weight matrix $\mathbf{W}$, pruning can be expressed by:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{W}_{\text{pruned}} = \mathbf{W} \odot \mathbf{M}, \quad \mathbf{M}_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } |W_{ij}| > \tau \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}

where $\tau$ is a threshold and $\odot$ denotes element wise multiplication.

\subsubsection{Knowledge Distillation}

Knowledge distillation trains a smaller ``student'' model which basically mimics the original ``teacher'' model \cite{hinton2015distilling}. While being effective, it requires the training access to the teacher model and in a lot of cases we might not have this access which makes it less suitable for post training compression.

\subsection{Post-Training Quantization for Edge Deployment}

PTQ's advantages: (1) no training is required (2) quantization takes minutes to hours (3) edge processors include specialized low precision arithmetic units and (4) memory efficiency, 4-bit quantization reduces footprint by 4$\times$ compared to FP16. These advantages make PTQ the most practical approach.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Post-Training Quantization Methods}

Major categories for PTQ methods are:

\subsubsection{INT8 Uniform Quantization}

Uniform quantization is to map floating-point values to uniformly spaced integer levels. For LLMs, LLM.int8() \cite{dettmers2022llm} introduced a mixed-precision decomposition which is used for identifying outlier feature dimensions that require higher precision:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}_{\text{normal}} \mathbf{W}_{\text{int8}} + \mathbf{X}_{\text{outlier}} \mathbf{W}_{\text{fp16}}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{X}_{\text{outlier}}$ contains dimensions with values exceeding a threshold. This approach very much preserves the accuracy for most models whilst giving us lesser memory usage.

Simple INT8 quantization gives us about 2$\times$ memory reduction with almost negligible accuracy loss, making it a reliable baseline approach.

\subsubsection{GPTQ: Optimal Brain Quantization}

GPTQ \cite{frantar2022gptq} adapts Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS) \cite{lecun1990optimal} for LLM quantization. Here we quantize weights sequentially meanwhile we also keep updating remaining weights to compensate for the error:

\begin{equation}
\delta_{\mathbf{W}} = -\frac{w_q - w}{[\mathbf{H}^{-1}]_{qq}} \cdot \mathbf{H}^{-1}_{:,q}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{H}$ is the Hessian matrix of the layer's output, $w$ is the original weight, $w_q$ is the quantized weight, and $q$ indexes the quantized weight position. The update $\delta_{\mathbf{W}}$ adjusts unquantized weights to minimize output error.

GPTQ results in impressive performance. But in this algorithm we have to compute inverse Hessian which has $O(d^3)$ complexity for a layer with $d$ as inputs, and this is addressed through Cholesky decomposition and lazy batch updates. Whilst it's effective, it is computationally expensive in nature.

\subsubsection{AWQ: Activation-Aware Weight Quantization}

AWQ has a unique approach to quantization, it protects the most important weights based on their activation magnitudes, it's an optimization over the standard quantization loss function and this results in super high accuracy:

\begin{equation}
s^* = \argmin_{s} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{W}, s), \quad \mathcal{L} = \sum_{i,j} \left| \mathbf{X}_{i,j} \cdot (w_{i,j} - \hat{w}_{i,j}) \right|
\end{equation}

where $s$ is a per-channel scaling factor, $\mathbf{X}$ represents activations from calibration data, and the scaling is applied as:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{W}} = \text{quant}(\mathbf{W} \cdot \text{diag}(s)) \cdot \text{diag}(s)^{-1}
\end{equation}

Due to its lossless nature, AWQ received the Best Paper Award at MLSys 2024.

\subsubsection{INT4 Weight-Only Quantization}

In INT4 weight-only quantization, we apply quantization to 4 bits without any compensation for errors; this results in a lot of accuracy loss especially for larger models---the larger the model, the greater the accuracy loss.

However, for smaller models <4B params, INT4 can strike a good balance between memory and accuracy loss.

\subsection{Quantization for Edge Deployment}

Several studies have explored LLM quantization for edge scenarios:

\textbf{llama.cpp} \cite{llamacpp2023} introduced GGUF format with various quantization schemes (Q4\_0, Q4\_K\_M, etc.) optimized for CPU inference. Their block quantization approach balances compression and accuracy.

\textbf{MLC-LLM} \cite{mlcllm2023} provides a compilation based approach for deploying quantized LLMs across diverse hardware platforms, including mobile devices and Apple Silicon.

\textbf{Huang et al.} \cite{huang2025llms} evaluated LLM inference on edge devices using Ollama, measuring energy efficiency across 28 quantized models on Raspberry Pi 4.

\textbf{Benazir and Lin} \cite{benazir2025profiling} profiled LLM inference on Apple Silicon from a quantization perspective, identifying optimal configurations for the M-series architecture.

Our work extends these studies by providing a comparison of inference frameworks (Ollama and MLX) specifically for recent small language models (Qwen3.5 series and Phi-3 Mini) with energy measurements on Apple Silicon.

\subsection{Small Language Models}

At the edge, we need SLMs due to the hardware limitations:

\textbf{Phi Series} \cite{javaheripi2023phi2, abdin2024phi3}: Microsoft's Phi models show us that if we carefully curate training data, we can enable small models to match the performance of larger models. Phi-3 Mini (3.82B parameters) gets strong results while maintaining a super compact footprint.

\textbf{Qwen Series} \cite{qwen2025technical}: Alibaba's Qwen 3.5 series of models include dense and Mixture-of-Expert architectures from 0.8B to 397B parameters. Qwen3.5 introduces a hybrid reasoning with thinking and non-thinking modes with GQA attention and dual-mode inference. Plus they are multimodal in nature, meaning they can support images as well. Furthermore, their smaller footprint and high accuracy among many benchmarks make them good candidates for deployment.

\subsection{Energy Measurement in ML}

Several tools have been developed for measuring energy consumption in ML workloads:

\textbf{CodeCarbon} \cite{codecarbon2023}: It's a python package that tracks emissions by monitoring CPU, GPU, and RAM power consumption. This also integrates with regional carbon intensity data and supports both online and offline modes.

\textbf{RAPL (Running Average Power Limit)}: This is the intel's interface for measuring processor power consumption, and it's widely used in ML energy studies.

\textbf{Hardware Power Meters}: they provide ground truth measurements but also require physical instrumentation.

In Apple Silicon energy measurement is challenging due to the unified memory architecture and limited software interfaces. We are going to address this through Apple's powerlog framework and CodeCarbon's macOS support.

\subsection{Models}

    \item \textbf{Qwen3.5-0.8B}: 873M parameters, optimized for extreme efficiency
    \item \textbf{Qwen3.5-2B}: 2.27B parameters, balanced size-performance trade-off
    \item \textbf{Qwen3.5-4B}: 4.66B parameters, more accurate model with higher resource requirements
\end{itemize}

Key features: GQA attention, RoPE embeddings, SwiGLU activation and dual-mode reasoning (thinking/\allowbreak non-thinking). All Qwen3.5 experiments conducted via both Ollama and MLX frameworks.

\subsubsection{Phi-3 Mini}

Phi-3 Mini \cite{abdin2024phi3} is a 3.82-billion parameter model from Microsoft's Phi-3 family: 3072 hidden dimensions, 32 attention heads, 32 layers, 128K context length. Phi-3 Mini experiments were conducted via both Ollama and MLX frameworks.

\subsection{Hardware Platform}

All experiments are conducted on Apple Silicon hardware. The M4 Pro's unified memory architecture eliminates CPU--GPU data transfer overhead and enables efficient on-device LLM inference. Full hardware specifications are listed in Section~\ref{sec:hw-setup}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluate each model and quantization configuration along three primary axes: inference latency, memory footprint, and energy consumption. Each metric is measured over 10 runs after a warmup pass.

\subsubsection{Latency}

We measure inference latency for generating 128 tokens with 256-token input context:

\begin{equation}
\text{Latency} = \frac{\text{Total Generation Time}}{\text{Number of Tokens}}
\end{equation}

We report mean and standard deviation over 10 runs after warmup.

\subsubsection{Memory Footprint}

We measure peak memory consumption during inference, including:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item Model weights (quantized or full-precision)
    \item KV cache for attention
    \item Activation buffers
    \item Runtime overhead
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Task Accuracy}

We evaluate accuracy retention under quantization using MMLU \cite{hendrycks2021mmlu}, a standard benchmark covering 57 subjects. We sample 100 questions across 10 subjects spanning STEM, humanities, social sciences, and other domains. Rather than parsing generated text---which is unreliable for models with thinking modes---we use \textbf{logit-based scoring}: for each question, we compare the model's next-token log-probabilities for tokens A, B, C, and D, selecting the highest. This method is deterministic, guarantees zero unparseable responses, and aligns with the evaluation methodology used by lm-evaluation-harness \cite{eval-harness}.

\subsubsection{Energy Consumption}

We measure energy using CodeCarbon \cite{codecarbon2023} integrated with Apple's power management framework:

\begin{equation}
E_{\text{inference}} = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} P(t) \, dt
\end{equation}

where $P(t)$ is instantaneous power consumption. We report energy in Joules per inference and compute energy efficiency as tokens per Joule.

\subsection{Experimental Limitations}

Our experiments revealed several important limitations for edge inference on Apple Silicon:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Energy measurement}: CodeCarbon provides estimates based on power models rather than direct hardware sensor readings; absolute energy values should be treated as approximations.

    \item \textbf{Token count}: Benchmarks use 100-token generation runs; energy and throughput characteristics may differ for longer sequences or streaming workloads.

    \item \textbf{Single device}: All results are from a single M4 Pro unit; chip-to-chip variation and thermal throttling under sustained load are not characterized.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Recommendations for Practitioners}

Based on our experimental findings:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Model selection}: Qwen3.5-0.8B with MLX INT4 provides exceptional throughput (213.11 tok/s) and energy efficiency (4.4 tok/J).

    \item \textbf{Framework choice}: Use MLX for all models on Apple Silicon, with speedups from 6.8$\times$ (Phi-3 Mini) to 13.4$\times$ (Qwen3.5-4B) vs Ollama.

    \item \textbf{Energy savings}: MLX INT4 provides 6.7$\times$ to 19.0$\times$ energy savings compared to Ollama.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What This Means for Green AI}

Our Apple Silicon M4 Pro experiments reveal some insights:

\subsubsection{Why Apple Silicon Works Well}

Apple's unified memory architecture gives it natural advantages for LLM inference:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item No GPU-CPU data transfer bottleneck
    \item 24GB unified memory accommodates all tested models in quantized form
    \item Metal Performance Shaders enable 213.11 tok/s for Qwen3.5-0.8B INT4
    \item Framework choice significantly impacts performance (MLX 7.8$\times$ faster than Ollama for Qwen3.5-0.8B)
\end{itemize}



\subsubsection{Accuracy--Efficiency Trade-off}

Our MMLU evaluation reveals an important practical insight: INT4 quantization on Apple Silicon delivers 2.0$\times$--3.8$\times$ speedup and 2.2$\times$--3.9$\times$ energy savings while losing at most 1 percentage point of accuracy for models $\geq$3.8B parameters. For smaller models, the trade-off is steeper---Qwen3.5-0.8B loses 5 points and Qwen3.5-2B loses 8 points---suggesting that practitioners should prefer models with at least 3--4B parameters when accuracy is critical for edge deployment.

\subsubsection{Where Energy Goes}

CodeCarbon measurements reveal a clear pattern:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item Model loading energy dominates first-time deployment
    \item Cached models show significantly lower energy footprint
    \item Apple M4 Pro estimated power: 42.5W CPU + 6W RAM (CodeCarbon model)
\end{itemize}

Energy efficiency varies with model size and quantization:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item Qwen3.5-0.8B INT4: 4.4 tokens/Joule (most efficient)
    \item Qwen3.5-4B INT4: 1.5 tokens/Joule
    \item Phi-3 Mini FP16: 0.5 tokens/Joule
    \item Phi-3 Mini INT4: 1.8 tokens/Joule
    \item INT4 quantization improves energy efficiency by 2.2$\times$ to 3.9$\times$ over FP16
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

Despite strong results, several factors constrain the generalizability of our findings.

\subsubsection{Hardware Specificity}

Our results apply specifically to Apple Silicon M4 Pro:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item NVIDIA GPUs may show different memory savings
    \item Mobile NPUs have different quantization support
    \item Results may not generalize to other Apple Silicon variants
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Model Selection}

We tested four models across two families; other architectures may behave entirely differently:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item Qwen3.5 thinking mode (extended reasoning) not evaluated
    \item Models with different training procedures may respond differently
    \item Very large models (>10B) were not evaluated
\end{itemize}



\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions deserve further work:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Cross-Architecture Evaluation}: Extend experiments to NVIDIA GPUs, mobile NPUs, and embedded processors.

    \item \textbf{Dynamic Quantization}: Exploring dynamic bit-width selection based on layer sensitivity.

    \item \textbf{Task-Specific Optimization}: Do different tasks need different quantization strategies?

    \item \textbf{Combined Techniques}: What happens when you combine quantization with distillation or structured pruning?

    \item \textbf{Carbon-Aware Deployment}: Integrating real-time carbon intensity data with quantization decisions could push energy savings further.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We evaluated inference frameworks for deploying LLMs on Apple Silicon edge devices. Testing Qwen3.5 series (Q-0.8B, 2B, 4B parameters) and Phi-3 Mini (3.82B parameters) on an M4 Pro with 24GB unified memory, we found:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Quantization benefits}: Within MLX, INT4 provides 2.0$\times$ to 3.8$\times$ speedup over FP16 with 3.5$\times$--3.6$\times$ memory reduction and 2.2$\times$ to 3.9$\times$ energy savings. Qwen3.5-0.8B achieves 213.11 tok/s with INT4 vs 104.69 tok/s with FP16.

    \item \textbf{Framework comparison}: MLX INT4 outperforms Ollama Q4\_K\_M by 6.8$\times$ to 13.4$\times$ in throughput and provides 6.7$\times$ to 19.0$\times$ energy savings across all models.

    \item \textbf{Practical deployment}: All tested models with MLX INT4 fit within 2.5GB memory, enabling deployment on devices with 8GB RAM while maintaining high throughput.

    \item \textbf{Accuracy retention}: MMLU evaluation confirms INT4 preserves 92--99\% of FP16 accuracy for models $\geq$3.8B parameters (Table~\ref{tab:mmlu_accuracy}), with Qwen3.5-4B and Phi-3 Mini losing only 1 percentage point each.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why INT4 Quantization Excels}

INT4 quantization reduces memory bandwidth requirements by 4$\times$ compared to FP16, enabling more weights to fit in cache hierarchy and reducing memory-bound stalls. For the models tested, this translates to 2.0$\times$--3.8$\times$ throughput improvement (Table~\ref{tab:latency}) with minimal accuracy degradation---MMLU accuracy drops by only 1 percentage point for models $\geq$3.8B parameters (Table~\ref{tab:mmlu_accuracy}).

\subsection{Why MLX Outperforms Ollama}

MLX's direct INT4 implementation leverages Metal Performance Shaders for native execution, whereas Ollama's GGUF-based block quantization introduces dequantization overhead during inference. This architectural difference produces 6.8$\times$--13.4$\times$ higher throughput and 6.7$\times$--19.0$\times$ energy savings across all models (Table~\ref{tab:framework_energy}).

\subsection{Real-World Impact}

Our findings enable several practical applications:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{On-device assistants}: With 2.5GB maximum memory footprint, models run entirely on iPhones/iPads without cloud connectivity.
    \item \textbf{Offline inference}: Field researchers can deploy LLMs in remote areas with 4.4 tokens/Joule efficiency (Qwen3.5-0.8B INT4).
    \item \textbf{Green computing}: Organizations can reduce inference energy by 6.7$\times$--19.0$\times$ by choosing MLX over Ollama.
    \item \textbf{Edge deployment}: All tested models fit within 8GB RAM, enabling IoT and embedded system deployment.
\end{itemize}

As data center energy consumption approaches 945 TWh annually, edge deployment will surely play a massive role in the upcoming future. Choosing the correct framework would impact performance by a lot.
